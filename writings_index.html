American Man

American man sat hunched over on a park bench. American Man’s face was a pale white, his features accented a candy red. He had great big round eyes that held tiny pinpoint pupils suspended in oceans of pure white. American Man’s tears streamed down his face and made small red pools where they gathered on the concrete below him. American Man was crying because he was sad. American Man had never been sad before.

A small child ran up beside him and snatched his wallet from the back of his pants pocket. The thief blew a raspberry in the direction of his victim as he fled the crime scene. American Man didn’t so much as flinch. He didn’t have any money anyway. What’s a wallet worth with no money in it?

American Man’s father had been a wealthy man. American Man was bequeathed a handsome inheritance but quickly blew it on such frivolities as cheese burgers, scratch-off cards, as seen on TV products, and so on. It would take an act of God to lift him from his state of anguish because American Man was wholly irrational.

Naturally, the reason American Man was sad was because he had no money. That much is rational. Never in his wildest dreams, though, did it occur to him that someday his profligacy might cause him to go broke. After all, his inheritance was handsome. It only made sense to him that he could go on spending and spending and spending to his heart’s desire without fear of any comeuppance. The only thing that ever meant anything to him was the fact that he was the son of a man of great wealth and importance. Not only was that man dead, but his wealth was too.

So, here was American Man, red-faced and disgraced, reduced to a hollow log on his bench. After a while like this, he felt it was probably time to get going. Where to he had no idea because he was served an eviction notice earlier that morning and his home was now in the process of being foreclosed. When he got up, he took no more than three steps before he was surrounded by a group of diminutive and disfigured snarling men who had emerged from the bushes next to him. They wore tattered rags for clothes and spoke what sounded like an unintelligible dialect of English in low pitched nasal grunts that were more like the cries of a camel than any language spoken by humans.

The most porcine of the bunch, the one American Man assumed to be their leader, held in his hand a metal rod affixed to a small round base. At the top of this rod dangled a piece of loose electrical wire that sputtered and sparked every so often. The rod was thrust in American Man’s direction and then the men started to form a circle around him. They stood there entranced, holding out their arms and pointing at him while they hummed something he couldn’t make out.

A few confusing minutes went by until American Man reached out his hand and grabbed the rod. This gesture shook them from their stupor and sent the strange little fellows into raptures. They hooted and hollered and ran all around American Man slapping each other merrily on the back. Although he had no clue as to why they might be doing this, American Man started to feel a bit better about himself. He took his hand off the rod and the men went immediately back into their trance. He straightened himself and once again took hold of the rod. Same story. Shouting, screaming, laughing, all of it.

American Man went on with this for a considerable length of time. Each time he grasped that peculiar device, they went delirious. Each time he let go, they froze like statues. Each time he felt better and better. He stood there triumphantly as his new acolytes went mad around him, went mad for him. American Man was back. American Man was powerful. American Man smiled.

Michaels Story

When I was six years old I drew Michaels. Now I am 23 years old and I still do Michaels but in a different way. Michaels can be anything. I assign to Michaels certain characteristics that they’re allowed to be. Michaels can be melancholic. Michaels can be normal or weird. Michaels can be bald but can also have some hair. Most Michaels are bald though and they don’t really have bodies, so they are sort of just a face and a head. The primordial Michaels used to have legs that stuck out from their heads kind of like blades of grass. I imagined them as spiky, pointy, and uncomfortable. Michaels don’t really have appendages anymore because they are more refined. It's less refined to have appendages. I didn’t ever let them have hands because they might grab something that they weren’t allowed to grab. I made a Michael yesterday. Michael has a long nose, a long face. Michael has a large, sort-of misshapen head. Michael has a scowl plastered on his face but his eyes are scared. Michael is probably scared this time. I hope that maybe Michael can try not to be so scared but that is the way he is right now so he’ll probably be that way for a while. Michael is also bald because most Michaels are bald. I considered adding a hair piece to Michael but it just didn’t feel right so he’s got a chrome dome, a pate, a shiny head. Michael’s going to go in the oven soon. Hot! He needs to dry off before he can do that. He could explode in the oven if he’s not dry. I hope Michael will be okay because he felt so nice in my hands. I wonder if Michael liked or somehow knew what my touch meant. Michael, please understand. It’s all that you can do. Don’t be scared, Michael. I love you, Michael.

Bench Sampler

I am the Bench Sampler. I went out the other day and rode my bike to the park in order to sample some benches. This park is really quite beautiful. The main attraction of the thing is a man-made swimming pool dug from the creek that runs through town. The pool is lined inside and out with concrete, and at the bottom, it gets slimy with green algae because the sun bakes its slow flowing water. When you stand at the bottom of the pool, you feel the wisps of the algae caress your feet gently. Some people enjoy this feeling while others do not. I am undecided on my feelings about the wisps.

I was not at the park for the green algae; I wanted to sit on the benches because I am the Bench Sampler. The first bench that I sat on was not actually a bench, unless you count the entire earth as one large bench that billions of people can occasionally sit on. That would be ridiculous, though. I sat underneath a tree a few hundred feet away from the concrete lined walkway of the man-made pool that was dug from the river that runs through town. The grass looked much sparser than the last time I was there and I worried that if I sat down on the damp exposed dirt my favorite pair of pants that I happened to be wearing would get irreparably stained. I sat down anyway. It’s just dirt. I pulled out my favorite novel, Kurt Vonnegut’s Cat’s Cradle, and began reading. Soon enough however, I was pestered by flying insects which became a recurring issue throughout my bench sampling. I got tired of them hovering over my head and behind my back and buzzing in my ears, so I picked myself up and hopped on my bike to find a new bench.

The next one I sat on was close to the pool. I immediately knew this bench was absolutely no good because there wasn’t any shade. Whenever I’m reading outside and there isn’t any shade, my field of vision gets smaller and the edges of my sight turn into a vignette. This is uncomfortable for me and causes me to feel like everything is wrong. I still sat on the bench for a while even though it was less than ideal. To my left and slightly behind me were two older women sitting underneath a tree in lawn chairs. They made quiet, unintelligible conversation next to me. It was probably unintelligible because I didn’t really want to listen to them on account of my wanting to read Cat’s Cradle by Kurt Vonnegut. To my right were two girls sitting on the edge of the pool with their feet dangling into the water. After a couple minutes, they decided to wade in the water. I figured that the water was probably pretty cold because they began shouting loudly shortly after entering. This sort of bothered me because I was trying to read but I think that maybe it shouldn’t because people like to do fun and adventurous things like wading through cold water and yelling. Either way, I had had enough of this bench and moved on.

The bench that followed was the one I originally desired when I got to the park. Upon arrival, there was what seemed to be a couple sitting together having lunch. I envied their bench because I conceived it to be the most optimal for what I wanted to do, that being reading Kurt Vonnegut’s Cat’s Cradle. It wasn’t too shady, nor was it too bright. There weren’t any bushes or long grasses that tend to attract swarms of those loathed flying insects. I thought I’d finally lucked into the best bench and that all of my previous failures would be rectified. It turned out, however, that this bench wasn’t what I’d cracked it up to be. The problems of the previous benches persisted. It was too hot, too buggy, too sunny. And, on top of all of this, the couple that sat on it before me left a mess of food on the ground that was unpleasant to look at. I felt frustrated, but as the Bench Sampler, I couldn’t be too upset. What else is a Bench Sampler to do besides sample benches? What I learned from this bench was that it is probably best not to get your hopes up too high.

When I sat down on the penultimate bench, I quickly realized that something had gone terribly wrong. My water bottle was slightly open in my bag and had begun to leak, soaking the edges of my books. I wasn’t too angry about this because it was my fault. I acknowledged that I made a mistake and tried not to be upset at myself. Books are just paper and I’m more of a thing that has other things that make up one large thing that is my whole self. Selves are fragile so it’s best to be nice to them. This bench was the best one so far. It was actually shady and I felt comfortable for the first time. I wasn’t able to fully absorb myself into my reading though because I kept getting distracted by people walking by. Every few lines, I would look up and see someone or a group of people pass me by and I wondered what they were thinking and feeling. I’m sure some of these people wondered the same about me. The last thing that I witnessed on this bench was a little girl having a meltdown while her mom pushed her in her stroller and her dad trailed behind holding a McDonald’s happy meal box. She made horrific shrieks and her mom tried to console her. Her mom kept saying to her, “You want to go to the park? We’re already at the park! If you stop crying we’ll go to the park,” in a gentle but urgent tone. This was contradictory and slightly funny to me. Why would she be crying that she wanted to go to the park if she was already at the park? I thought it was nice that her mom was being so patient with her and then I thought about how I would handle that situation. Kids are strange but so are grownups. I think that ultimately kids just want what grownups want but in a little bit of a different way.

After this scene, I got fed up with the bugs again. It seemed like wherever I went, they followed, which could be nice if they weren’t bugging me. I guess that’s why they call them bugs. It is sort of like a chicken and egg situation, though. Was “bugging” a previously established term that was applied to insects or did it emanate from them? Anyway, I moved to the last bench of the day and sat down. I started reading Rousseau’s essay on political economy this time instead of Kurt Vonnegut’s Cat’s Cradle. He talks about individual families, large families (political societies), the “general will,” the magistrates, sovereignty, law, right, and other lofty stuff like that. He says that when the will of an individual aligns with that of the population of a society at large, and if virtue is instilled in a people, a prosperous political body emerges. I think that’s generally true but how do we best instill virtue in a people? What is virtuous? Who decides what is virtuous? After reading a couple pages of Rousseau, I felt like I was done sampling benches so I packed my things up, got on my bike, and rode back home.
